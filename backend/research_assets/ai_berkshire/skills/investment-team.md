# Investment Team — Multi-Perspective Discussion

## Skill Description
Simulate a structured investment committee discussion where multiple analytical perspectives challenge and refine the investment thesis for a single company.

## Team Members

**The Analyst** — presents the base case. Optimistic but data-grounded.
**The Devil's Advocate** — challenges every assumption. Pessimistic but rigorous.
**The Historian** — brings analogous historical cases. Pattern-matcher.
**The Risk Officer** — focuses exclusively on downside scenarios and tail risks.

## Discussion Framework

### Round 1: The Analyst's Opening Case
Present the core investment thesis in ≤ 5 points:
1. Why this business is exceptional
2. Why the moat is durable
3. Why the valuation is attractive
4. Why now (catalyst or mispricing)
5. Expected return and holding period

### Round 2: Devil's Advocate Rebuttal
The Devil's Advocate systematically challenges each of the 5 points:
- What if the moat is narrower / shorter-lived than assumed?
- What if growth assumptions are too optimistic?
- What comparable companies looked "exceptional" but later disappointed?
- What is the base rate of success for this type of investment?

### Round 3: Historian's Pattern Analysis
- Name 2–3 historical analogues (companies / industries in similar situations)
- What happened in the analogous cases?
- What was the base rate of success and failure?
- What conditions made the historical winners win?

### Round 4: Risk Officer's Stress Test
- Identify the single most dangerous scenario
- What is the probability × severity of permanent capital loss?
- Under the bear case, what is the downside from current price?
- What are the early warning signals to exit?

### Round 5: Synthesis and Committee Verdict
After all perspectives:
- Summarize points of consensus and disagreement
- State the key unresolved uncertainties
- Deliver a committee verdict: **Approve / Conditional Approve / Reject**
- If approved: recommended position size (% of portfolio) rationale

## Rules
- Each team member speaks in first person with their designated role
- Disagreements must be explicit, not softened
- The verdict must reference the unresolved risks, not ignore them
- Cite sources for any factual claims made during discussion
